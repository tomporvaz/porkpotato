11/30/2018 Auburn Overlook to Old Stage Coach Road Trail (5mi)

Arriving early with plenty of time ahead of me, I decided to park at the bottom of the hill. I really like to do things the hard way! The road was a nice beginning to this trek. It was a long 1.2 miles switch back through the woods up the hill with a nice flat gravel surface. I met a game warden on my way to the top who informed me that it was open season for rifles. I was prepared with my orange shirt.

I met many hunters along the road and a I saw a couple on the trail too. This was nothing that concearned me, and I hardly heard any more gun shots then I am used to.

At the top of the road was a nice parking lot and Aubun Overlook. Note: This is a nice short hike or drive for a fairly rewarding overlook. It has a large rock slide that affords a nice view looking north. I enjoyed a quiet meditation here in July, and again on this hike.

During my meditation, it began to rain, but a quick look at my weather app confirmed that it was nothing to fear. I pushed on for relatively flat grade for most of my journey with many stretches full of rocks. The top of a mountain is a different place once all the leaves have fallen. Lots of sky showed through the forest without the leaves to block it. I could really see just how thin the top of the mountain is.

The forrest had a nice variety with a lot of disiduous growth some pine and areas of thick rhodedendron's. The last leg of the journey was the hardest. First, I passed a spring which I thought indicated only 1 mile remaining. This final mile, however, included a steep desent, many rocks, and a lot of streams. The changes of scenery was rewarding, but the persistent light rain had begun to soak through making me a little colder then I should be in 34 degree weather.

I finished the steep desent, ate my lunch qucikly under a tree and hurried back to the parking lot for my drive home.

7/28/2017 Hawk Mountain Road to The Pinnicale (10 mi)

The trail from Hawk Mountain Road was very welcoming with a sea of thick green ferns as far as the eye could see. The forest is abundant with growth in July. I thought I found chantrelles and the rasberries had a few scraps left. The ascent was gradual, and the terrain was much less rocky than other sections I have hiked in this area. Much of the trail follows dirt roads. This was an easy hike that allowed me to cover a whole 10 miles without too much complaining.

Hiking up a gradual slope I could hear a babbling brook. As if the sound wasn't enough, a small valley opened up to display a goregous stream several yards below me. Yonder up the hill I lunched at the Pinnicale. The Pinnicale has been claimed to be the nicest overlook on the AT in PA, but I think they all look the same. Nevertheless, it was a nice spot to stop. Vultures soared beside me as I ate and looked out over the farms. After this, I walked back to my car.

5/22/2017 Jacksonville Snyders Road to Hawk Mountain Road (19 mi)

Long overdue for some wilderness adventure time, I finnally got out to the woods for a backpacking trip. I saved up comp time from final exams and graduation to go on this adventure. Leaving home a little before 7:00 AM, I hit traffic on I-76. I left my car at a parking lot on a dirt road intersecting the AT on top of the mountain.

THe hike began quickly on a rocky dirt road. The terrain was relatively flat for a long while and eventually encountered some ups and downs. This section of trail is loaded with water (3) and vistas (3). Of course I didn't see much at the vistas during this soggy hike. Visibility was low a top the mountain, but I did make good time getting to Dan's Pulpit. Starting aroung 9:45 am and arriving at Dan's Pulpit around 1:15 pm, I figured I kept close to a 30 minute mile even with my pack.

I saw lots of thru hikers, and got rained out of my lunch. A long desent from Dan's Pulpit revealed many camping sites. THe top of the mountain had a few sites, but most of the terrain was too rocky. Once at Hawk Mountain Road, I had to hike left down the road to a shelter for water.

The shelter was very nice. with 4 walls, a door, and a privy. This looke to be a part of someones private property. It even had running water and electricity.

So after filling up on water I headed back up the mountain. Quite fatigued, I stopped at the first campsite I saw. I rested up for a bit, then gained enough energy to push onward. I hiked an additional 1.75 miles up the hill. This allowed me to have a speedy return in the morning so I could pick up my kids from daycare on time. My total for the day was 11.25 miles which I think is about my limit with a pack.

2/27/2017 Palmerton Lehigh Valley to Lehigh Furnace Gap

Mountain climbing was long overdue, and this trip was great hike to get back in the groove. Starting in Palmerton, I hiked south to lehigh furnace gap. The assent out of the lehigh valley was steep but manageable. The woods were thick. Once up the hill I came to a spring, a shelter, and the first blue side trail. Not much reason to go on from there.

The trail is nice with some rocks but more dirt then other sections of the AT in Pennsylvannia. The trail is pretty flat and mostly wooded. At some point the trees become sparse, and views begin to appear. In the winter, I could see right through the leafless trees down on the farmlands below.

I ate my lunch on a nice out crop of rocks overlooking the fields. Not too windy or cold, so I got to hang. A little further down the trail and I turned around at the next dirt road.

The adventure back was a little more exciting and more challenging. I took both side blue trails on the return. The first was all rocks (more like boulders). The walk became much more technical and offered plenty of great views. The challenge was welcomed, and the feeling of barreness was worth it. The next blue trail offered the barreness and views sans challenging rock slide. The view was especially interesting overlooking towns, highways, and the Lehigh River. I saw a train that looked like a toy from that height and all the nooks of the river. The desent was long. I didn't see my first person until the end. THe challenge was worth the solitude and peace. This soreness is one I am proud of.

12/28/2016 Wind Gap to Fox Gap Parking Area Rt 191 (Northbound)

I set out early in the morning for what was bound to be a long day. The trail was 8.5 miles long in one direction, so I knew I would need at least 9 hours to complete this stretch twice, up and back. The trail starts off rather steep for only the first 50 ft or so. I continued to climb over the next mile until I reached the top of the hill. The mountain seemed wide, and rocky. I have never missed a turn on a trail so much as this one on account of my focus was constanly on my footing. Rarley able to focus on the trail blazes, I followed a supposed trail several feet before I realized I was lost. I found my way back! I also startled several groups of dear in the same manner. Unaware of my sorroundings, I suddenly heard dear leaping in the opposite direction of me. I got a good look!

On top of the mountain I noticed another mountain to the north of my position. This is the little offset, and apparently the mountian spits in to two pieces. The trail continues though different types of vegetation. Some areas with a lot of undergrowth, other areas almost barren, and occasionally there unmelted pockets of snow. I crossed several small rock slides, and eventually made it up to Wolf Rocks!

Wolf Rocks was spectacular! The rock formatoins were interesting, sorrounded by laurels, and providing a splendid view over the trees. There aren't many views along this stretch of trail, but the sights from Wolf Rock make up for the lack. Wolf rock stretches for a couple hundred feet, and it is nicely integrated with the landscape. I would have spent more time on this cliff had the wind and temperature not been so fierce. I did spend some time in aww, gathering pictures. Close to the nothern edge of this section, I think this spot would be great for a day hike. As finished the the trail, I met many folks who agreed and had been hiking the short walk Wolf Rock from 191.

I ate a quick lunch at the end of the trail and began my return trek. Leaving aroung 1230pm, I thought I had ample time to make it back to my car by dusk. Indeed, I was right, however, I did not have ample energy. The trail was long and I was tired. I knew that continuos movement was the only thing that would get me back. My feet were very sore, and my ankles were screaming. I broke in my new pair of Asolos on this trip, and although my feet complained, I think these shoes were more to thank than to blame. I laced my boots up good a tight to protect my ankles, and although they were sore, they were not injured. I think hike 17 miles in a day is just to much!

12/16/2016 Wind Gap Rt 33 to Leroy Smith Shelter Powerlines (Southbound)

This hike was long overdue and squeezed into the second half of my day. Arriving to the parking lot around around 1:30pm, I knew that I only had 4 hours of hiking before the sun went down. This 4 mile hike should have easily fit into this 4 hour window, but darkness was close on my heals. In an unussually cold week in December (High of 23F), I set out to Wind Gap desperate to get some nature time. THe parking lot for this trip is on the north bound side of the road and the south bound trail is several hundred feet up the road and under the overpass. The trail starts next to Rt 33, a fairly busy divided highway. The trail climbs steeply up the slope of the Wind Gap, but you hardly feel the incline on the many swithchbacks. You quickly rewarded with a lookpout to the north. For no apparent reason, the trail makes a sharp left down the south side of the mountain a little to walk a trail across the slope. A little up and down never hurt anyone. After this final climb, you are greeted by another overlook, Hahns Lookout, showing the towns to the south and the farmlands all around. From here the trail stays on the top of the mountain, but not without some subtle elevation changes.

On top of the mountain, I encountered much more snow from earlier in the week. The snow was a treat covering some of the rocks on the trail, highlighting some tracks of rodents and beast alike, and providing a lovely crunch as meandered through the forrest. The tracks showed that very few people had walked this trail recently (I saw 3 people). I saw many human tracks, as well as a dog, some small rodents, bunnies, and deer. The snow is a fabulous place to practice some tracking. The snow was also quite beautiful, only covering the smaller rocks in some places and glistening in the sun where it had a chance to freeze and refreeze.

The pipeline is the first landmark you come to after Hahn's Lookout. It is somewhere in the second mile. It privides another outlook and plenty of space to have a picnic. Back in the woods the terrain remains relatively flat with many rocks to avoid. On this type of ground I look at my footing more often then I look up to enjoy the view. The top of the mountain is heavily wooded and mostly deciduous. I did see some large birch trees? and a confiferous tree occasionally.

My journey from the pipeline to my final destination, the powerlines, was hasty. Having reached the pipline around 2:50 PM, I knew needed to hurry. Three O'Clock my half way time, and I should have turned around. I didn't! This rebel hiked full throttle anxiously waiting for the powerlines to pop out of the above the tree line. I might have even done a brief jag in anticipation. With over 1.5 miles between the pipeline and powerlines, expecting to do it in less than a half hour was wishful. I made it to the powerlines by 3:30 PM, my next deadline to turnaround.

I was unsure of how long it would take me to return, and when exactly the light would disappear. I knew the sun set at 4:30 PM, but thankfully, twilight lasted until 5:00 PM! Having made it to Hahns' Lookout by 5:00 PM, I only had the steep switchback to accomplish in the dark. I managed and never used my flashlight. This last part of the trail was steep but the ground had few rocks to be cautious of. During twighlight and the final dark moments, the cities sparkled below with streetlights and many other luminesints. Looking from Hahn's Lookout, I enjoyed the lights of civilization, and the story they told of small sprawl of a rural town.
